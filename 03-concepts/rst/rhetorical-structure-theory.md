# Rhetorical Structure Theory
*Source: Mann, W. C., Matthiessen, C. M. I. M., & Thompson, S. A. (1992). Rhetorical structure theory and text analysis. In Discourse Description: Diverse Linguistic Analyses of a Fund-Raising Text, 39–78.*

## Core idea
Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST) is a theory of text structure. It explains what kinds of parts texts consist of and how those parts combine into larger units and, ultimately, into whole texts.

RST treats text structure as fundamentally relational: texts are composed of *functionally* significant parts, and these parts are linked together through recurrent rhetorical relations. Textual unity and coherence arise because the parts of a text are interpreted as *contributing to a single communicative purpose or intended effect on the reader*.

In a broader perspective, such approach to discourse and discourse analysis is aimed at contributing "to an account of how language can be used to communicate, i.e. a detailed description of how it contributes to the outcomes of interactions." (Mann et al., 1992, p. 40)

---

## 1. Text structure as relational and hierarchical
According to Mann, Matthiessen, and Thompson, texts are not just linear strings of sentences. They are organized hierarchically.

Key points:

- elementary parts combine into larger parts;
- larger parts combine into still larger parts;
- this process continues up to the level of the whole text.

Importantly, *the same general kinds of structural patterns can operate at different scales*. A relation that links two small spans can also help organize much larger portions of text.

The authors therefore argue that there is *a single set of structural patterns available across levels of text organization*. These are called RST schemas. Cf.:

> Within relational structure, RST assumes homogeneity: there is one set of structural patterns available for organizing the text at every scale, from the largest, an element of holistic structure (e.g. letter body, magazine article body, possibly the whole text...) down to the smallest scale (possibly a two-clause combination). (Mann et al., 1992, p. 43-44)

What is more, there seems to be a somewhat limited inventory of rhetoric relations that most (all?) texts in a culture draw on. Cf.:

> The set of text structuring relations is in principle open, so that additional previously unused relations can arise. However, the frequency of creation of new relations is extremely low, and for all but a kernel set the frequency of use of rare or unknown relations is also extremely low, so that text in a culture can be analyzed virtually entirely in terms of a small set of highly recurrent relations, the knowledge of which is shared in the culture. (Mann et al., 1992, p. 46)

So texts can be very different in content, but still be built from the same basic families of relations:

- elaboration
- evidence
- background
- condition
- cause/explanation
- contrast
- joint
- sequence
- etc.

This is one reason an "automated" RST parser can be used on a large corpus of texts:  we are assuming that these recurrent relation types can be counted and compared across texts.

 Mann, Matthiessen, and Thompson provide (p. 52) the following inventory of relations that they "have found useful":
 
 **Nucleus-Satellite Relations:**
 - Evidence
 - Justify
 - Antithesis
 - Concession
 - Circumstance
 - Solutionhood
 - Elaboration
 - Background
 - Enablement
 - Motivation
 - Volitional Cause
 - Non-volitional Cause
 - Volitional Result
 - Non-volitional Result
 - Purpose
 - Condition
 - Otherwise
 - Interpretation
 - Evaluation
 - Restatement
 - Summary
 
 **Multi-nuclear Relations**
 - Sequence
 - Contrast
 - Joint

## 2. The main structural principle: rhetorical relations
The principal structural pattern in multisentence text is relational. A relatively small set of recurrent relations is used to connect text spans and build larger textual units.

The most common type of relation in RST is the nucleus-satellite relation.

### Nucleus and satellite
This relation is asymmetric:

- the nucleus is the more central part;
- the satellite is the more peripheral or supporting part.

So not all parts of a text are equally important. Some parts carry the central communicative burden, while others support, justify, elaborate, motivate, or condition them.

These relations are *functional*. They are defined not simply by formal markers, but by the kinds of effects they are intended to produce, especially in relation to:

- the writer’s purpose,
- the writer’s assumptions about the reader,
- and the propositional organization of the text.

Cf.:

> "Text structuring relations are functional; the character that they all share can be stated in terms of the categories of effects that they produce. They can be described in terms of the purposes of the writer, the writer's assumptions about the reader, and certain propositional patterns in the subject matter of the text. (Mann et al., 1992, p. 44-45)

## 3. RST is pre-realizational
One of the most important theoretical points in the chapter is that RST relations do not strictly hold between raw word sequences as such.

Rather, the word sequences in a text are understood as realizations of more abstract entities:

- meanings,
- intentions,
- communicative functions.

So RST is, strictly speaking, pre-realizational: it describes how meanings and intentions are structured and combined, not how they are lexically or grammatically realized on the surface.

For convenience, the authors still speak of relations holding between spans of text. But the deeper claim is that these relations operate at the level of discourse meaning and intention.

This is why rhetorical relations:

- may be signaled by conjunctions or other surface markers,
- but do not depend on them,
- and may also be fully implicit.

So RST rejects the view that text-structuring relations are essentially lexical. It offers a way of describing discourse organization whether or not the relations are overtly marked.

## 4. Basic terms in RST

### Text span
A text span is any portion of text that:
- has an RST structure of its own,
- or is realized by a unit.

Units are typically clauses, cf.:

> Unit size is arbitrary in RST; in principle the units can be of any size from typical lexical items to entire paragraphs or larger. In our work on RST, however, we have found it useful to use units that have some relatively theory-neutral functional integrity. We have derived interesting results from considering the units to be roughly clauses, except that clausal subjects and objects and restrictive relative clauses are considered parts of their host clauses rather than separate units. (Mann et al., 1992, p. 51)

### Relation
A relation in RST is a relation between two text spans.

In a typical asymmetric relation:
- one span functions as the nucleus,
- the other as the satellite.

---

## 5. How an RST relation is defined
Each relation is defined using two main components:

### 1. Constraints
The definition includes constraints on:

- the nucleus,
- the satellite,
- and the combination of nucleus and satellite.

#### Constraints on the nucleus
These say what must be true, or plausibly true, about the **more central span** for this relation to make sense.

In plain English:

*What kind of thing is the nucleus doing, lacking, needing, or claiming?*

Example with **Evidence**: the nucleus is something the reader may not yet believe strongly enough (see: Mann et al., 1992, p. 48). That is a nucleus-side condition.

#### Constraints on the satellite
These say what must be true, or plausibly true, about the **supporting span**.

In plain English:

> What role is the supporting span able to play?

Again with **Evidence**: the satellite has to be something the reader already finds credible, or is likely to accept as credible. Otherwise it cannot function as evidence (Mann et al., 1992, p. 48).

#### Constraints on the combination
These are the most important and the easiest to miss.

They are about what happens when the two spans are taken **together**.

In plain English:

> If I combine these two spans, what relationship becomes plausible between them?

With **Evidence**, the combination constraint is that understanding or accepting the satellite should increase the reader’s belief in the nucleus (Mann et al., 1992, p. 48).

These constraints guide the analyst in determining whether a particular relation plausibly holds. In other words, a relation label is not assigned just because a conjunction appears (see ## 3 above). The analyst asks whether the nucleus, the satellite, and their combination fit the profile of that relation. RST relation definitions are explicitly built around those plausibility judgments.

### 2. Effect
Each relation definition also includes an Effect field.

This states:

- what effect the writer may plausibly be trying to produce by using the relation;
- and where that effect is located:
- in the nucleus alone,
- or in the nucleus-satellite combination.

This is a major strength of RST: every relation definition gives the analyst a plausible reason why a given part of the text is there.

In other words, RST tries to explain not only structure, but also:
- authorial intention,
- and the likely effect on the reader.

---

## 6. Interpretation and plausibility
RST analysis is interpretive, but not arbitrary.

The analyst has access to:
- the text,
- the context in which it was produced,
- and the cultural knowledge shared by writer and expected readers.

However, the analyst does not have direct access to the writer’s mind or to actual reader responses.

Therefore, judgments in RST are always plausibility judgments, not claims of certainty.

The logic is always something like:

It is plausible to the analyst that the writer intended this effect or that this relation holds.

This is especially important for the Effect field: the analyst judges whether it is plausible that the writer wanted to produce the specified effect on the reader.

---

## 7. Holistic structure vs relational structure

A very useful distinction in the chapter is the one between holistic structure and relational structure.

### Holistic structure
Some parts of a text belong to conventional genre form rather than to its rhetorical-relational organization.

In the case of a fund-raising letter (that the authors analyze in the chapter), these include things like:

- the letterhead,
- the date,
- the greeting,
- the closing,
- the postscript.

These are part of what the authors call the holistic structure of the letter.

### Relational structure
By contrast, the main body of the text can be analyzed in terms of rhetorical relations between spans.

So the body of the text belongs to the relational structure, while formulaic textual components like greeting and closing belong to the holistic structure.

This is a useful distinction because it prevents us from forcing every part of a genre into the same relational analysis.

## The Most Important Takeaways
1. A theory of text structure must explain both:

	- what parts texts have,
	- and how those parts combine.

2. Text structure is fundamentally relational.
3. Texts are hierarchical: smaller spans combine into larger spans.
4. The main organizing pattern is usually nucleus-satellite structure.
5. RST relations are functional, not merely formal.
6. RST operates at the level of meanings and intentions, not just surface wording.
7. Relations may be:

	- lexically or grammatically signaled,
	- or completely implicit.

10. The analyst works with plausibility judgments, not certainty.
11. Not all textual organization is rhetorical in the same sense: some parts belong to holistic genre structure rather than relational structure.
