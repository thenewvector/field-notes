# Construing EDUs: An SFL Perspective on RST Parsing

#rst #sfl #discourse #sem-segm #depr-rst
*Version 1.0 — 17 May 2026*

## The Core Intuition

Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL) offers a powerful semantic lens for discourse segmentation: the so-called "ideation base." By viewing text through this conceptual layer, we can better determine where the boundaries of Elementary Discourse Units (EDUs) must be drawn.

Instead of relying on strict syntactic rules (e.g., *"Is there a finite verb here?"*), the guiding question becomes:

> **Does this text span congruently realize a single "figure" of experience, or is it a sequence of distinct figures?**

In SFL, a figure models one quantum of change. It is built around:

- A **process**
- One or more **participants**
- Optionally, **circumstances**

**Example:** `He opened the door slowly.`

- `opened` = process
- `he` = participant
- `the door` = participant
- `slowly` = circumstance

Despite containing multiple experiential elements, this represents a single, unified figure. Therefore, from a discourse perspective, it constitutes exactly one EDU.

Parsing contradictions typically arise with "shrunken" or non-finite clauses, such as *opening bottles* in the sentence: *They developed a method for opening bottles*. Should this be a separate EDU? Based on the intuition above, it actually should be—and in some RST traditions, it is.

The rationale is that a figure does not have to be realized lexicogrammatically as a fully finished sentence with a finite verb. The absolute minimum requirement for a semantic figure is its "nucleus" -- a closely bonded configuration consisting of a **Process** and the core participant (the **Medium**) through which that process is realized. Because *opening bottles* retains this fundamental Process + Participant nucleus, it functions as a distinct, underlying figure of experience.

Recognizing this distinction between a formal syntactic clause and an underlying semantic figure of experience is crucial, as it perfectly illuminates why different RST parsing frameworks handle these structural edge cases in diverging ways.

## The Theoretical Foundation: Language, Semantics, and Metafunctions in SFL

To fully understand why SFL prioritizes this semantic "figure" over surface-level syntax, it is necessary to look at the broader theoretical architecture of the framework and how it views human cognition.

### Semantics, Lexicogrammar, and Metafunctions
Systemic Functional Linguistics (SFL) posits that semantics is the foundational "machinery" through which we make sense of the world. In this view, "knowing" or "understanding" is not an isolated psychological process, but rather the act of construing our experience into the semiotic categories provided by a language's grammar. SFL practitioners take a firm stance on this relationship, essentially arguing that language **is** cognition:

> We contend that the conception of 'knowledge' as something that exists independently of language, and may then be coded or made manifest in language, is illusory. All knowledge is constituted in semiotic systems, with language as the most central; and all such representations of knowledge are constructed from language in the first place. (Halliday & Matthiessen, 1999, p. 3).

This framework relies on a tight, "natural" connection between semantics (meaning) and lexicogrammar (wording). Rather than existing in isolation, "experience is construed ***twice*** in the content plane, once semantically and once lexicogrammatically" (Halliday & Matthiessen, 1999, p. 5). Because these two strata are naturally coupled, "patterns of wording reflect patterns of meaning" (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 27).

Consequently, while the semantic stratum cannot be observed directly, it can be systematically accessed and mapped through grammatical analysis. As Halliday and Matthiessen (1999) state, "we are taking the grammar as a point of departure for exploring the organization of semantics" (p. 28).

The wording itself (whether realized graphically or phonologically) is never a one-dimensional structure. Instead, it is seen in SFL as a "lamination" of three different metafunctions:

- **The ideational metafunction**, which serves to construe human experience and provides "a working theory of reality" (so we can make sense of it, as noted above). It is divided into two components: the **experiential**, which organizes our experience of the world into configurations of processes, participants, and circumstances, and the **logical**, which constructs the relationships between these processes.
- **The interpersonal metafunction**, which is concerned with enacting personal and social relationships. This is "language as action," providing the resources for interactants to engage in dialogue, exchange information or goods-and-services, and express their own attitudes, judgments, and appraisals.
- **The textual metafunction**, which plays an enabling role by organizing both ideational and interpersonal meanings into a coherent flow of discourse. It ensures that the text makes contact with its surrounding context by managing the thematic structure of messages, the flow of given and new information, and the overall cohesion of the language.

When viewed specifically through the lens of the **ideational metafunction**, the segmentation of a text can be understood in terms of how it realizes the phenomena of the 'ideation base'—the semantic resource language uses for construing our experience of the world. From this perspective, the flow of discourse is semantically organized into sequences of **figures**. Because a figure represents a single, unified quantum of change in our experience, it is arguably the most critical element of the ideation base for determining how to chunk text spans. Consequently, identifying these underlying figures provides the ideal theoretical foundation for defining the boundaries of the Elementary Discourse Units (EDUs) used in RST.

### Figures and Their Sequences

A figure in SFL is "a basic fragment of experience that embodies one quantum of change" (Halliday & Matthiessen, 1999, p. 128). Structurally, it is a semantic configuration consisting of three elements: a process unfolding through time, the participants directly involved in that process, and any attendant circumstances.

The most essential part of a figure is the **nucleus**.

- **The Components:** The nucleus is a closely bonded configuration formed by the **Process** (the temporal unfolding of the event) and the **Medium** (the key participant through which that process comes into existence).
- **The "Centre of Gravity":** Semantically, the nucleus acts as the focal point or "centre of gravity" around which the entire figure is organized. It represents a complementarity between a temporal action (the Process) and an atemporal, persisting entity (the Medium).
- **Actualization:** SFL posits that a process cannot occur without an element to translate it from the virtual to the actual; therefore, the Process is **actualized** by fusing with the Medium. Because of this essential bonding, the Medium is the only obligatory participant element in the clause.
- **Structural Foundation:** Together, the Process and Medium create a foundational "semantic field" that dictates the range of additional structural options—such as whether an external Agent or a Range can be added—available to the rest of the clause.

Grammatically, a figure is typically realized by a clause, serving to model human experience into specific domains of doing, happening, sensing, saying, being, or having. Basically, when we want to say something meaningful, we deploy figures and link them into sequences.

In the semantic unfolding of a text, adjacent figures are linked into immediate sequences. Grammatically, these sequences are realized by **clause complexes** -- or **clause nexuses**, when referring specifically to a single tactically related pair of clauses (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 441). While traditional grammar often categorizes these as compound or complex sentences, SFL explicitly treats them as univariately structured chains of interdependent clauses (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 437).

Within SFL's architecture, the relationships that speakers and writers establish between these figures belong to the **logical** mode of meaning. Together with the experiential mode, this forms the overarching **ideational** metafunction, which serves to construe human experience (Halliday & Matthiessen, 1999, p. 514).

Therefore, when clauses are joined together to form a clause complex, they are not merely placed side-by-side; they are connected by specific **logico-semantic relations** (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 428). At the most fundamental level, language provides two primary types of these relations to organize experience: **expansion** and **projection** (Halliday & Matthiessen, 1999, p. 106; Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 443).

**1. Expansion** Expansion links clauses that relate phenomena belonging to the same order of experience. In this relation, a secondary clause expands on the primary clause in one of three ways:

- **Elaborating:** The secondary clause expands the primary clause by restating it in other words, specifying it in greater detail, exemplifying it, or providing a comment.
- **Extending:** The secondary clause expands the primary clause by adding a new element, offering an alternative, or providing an exception.
- **Enhancing:** The secondary clause expands the primary clause by qualifying it with circumstantial features, such as time, place, cause, means, or condition.

**2. Projection** Projection links phenomena of one order of experience (the physical acts of saying or thinking) to phenomena of a higher, "second-order" semiotic reality (the content of what is said or thought). A secondary clause is projected through the primary clause as either:

- **Locution (wording):** The secondary clause is projected by a "verbal" process, representing the construction of wording, or what is _said_.
- **Idea (meaning):** The secondary clause is projected by a "mental" process (such as sensing or cognition), representing the construction of meaning, or what is _thought_.

Additionally, projection can involve different speech functions, meaning the projected clauses can be either **propositions** (exchanging information, such as statements and questions) or **proposals** (exchanging goods and services, such as commands and offers).

The logico-semantic relations (like cause, condition, or elaboration) do not just exist locally between clauses; they extend across the entire text, linking (rhetorical) paragraphs and large text spans. The difference is _how_ they are realized grammatically:

- **Locally**, these logico-semantic relations are realized by the logical mode of the grammar, building structural **clause complexes**. And this is where grammar stops in SFL -- the highest structural unit of grammar is the clause complex (what we traditionally call a sentence).
- **Globally**, since grammar cannot build structures large enough to encompass whole paragraphs, these exact same logico-semantic relations are realized by **cohesion** (specifically, conjunctions like _therefore, meanwhile, however_), which belongs to the **textual metafunction**.

This complementarity is exactly what RST captures. RST models the text semantically as a complex of rhetorical relations. When we build an RST tree, we are mapping out what would in SFL be logico-semantic relations that are realized _structurally_ through clause complexing at the local level (leaves of the tree/EDUs), and _cohesively_ through textual conjunctions at the global level (higher nodes in the tree addressing larger spans).

In other words, a text's overall rhetorical structure relies on a division of labor between the two systems as discussed in SFL: clause complexing does the local work of linking adjacent figures, and cohesive conjunction does the non-local or global work of linking larger text spans.

## Comparison of Two RST Traditions

This section presents a comparative analysis of two similar approaches to Rhetorical Structure Theory (RST) parsing: the framework established by [Stede et al. (2017)](https://www.sfu.ca/~mtaboada/docs/research/RST_Annotation_Guidelines.pdf) (associated with the SLSeg parser) and the [Georgetown University Multilayer (GUM) corpus guidelines](https://wiki.gucorpling.org/gum/rst) (which utilizes "enhanced RST", or eRST).

These two frameworks were selected for a side-by-side comparison because, while fundamentally similar, they diverge in critical edge cases regarding how they chunk Elementary Discourse Units (EDUs). These divergences perfectly illustrate the theoretical tension between strict syntactic rules and functional semantic weight.

The Stede et al. approach represents a stricter, "originalist" perspective. It deliberately adheres closely to the foundational RST proposals laid out by Mann and Thompson in 1988. In this framework, discourse segmentation is tightly bound to formal grammatical structures. Most notably, it operates on the strict principle that almost every EDU must contain a verb (Stede et al., 2017, p. 5). It maintains a hard line between syntax and discourse, treating adjunct clauses as discourse spans while strictly refusing to segment complement clauses -- even going so far as to keep reported speech and its reporting verb as a single unit.

In contrast, the GUM framework represents a slightly more "semantically aggressive" evolution of RST. Its baseline segmentation rules are rooted in the [2001 RST Discourse Treebank (RST-DT) guidelines](https://faculty.washington.edu/fxia/lsa2011/readings/RST-tagging-ref-manual.pdf) created by Carlson and Marcu. While GUM now parses text using eRST -- a formalism that introduces secondary, tree-breaking relations and categorizes signal tokens -- this enhancement is largely irrelevant to the initial chunking phase. As the GUM documentation notes, in terms of text segmentation, "GUM should have identical guidelines to RST-DT" ([Rhetorical Structure Theory Annotation - eRST, n.d.](https://wiki.gucorpling.org/gum/rst)).

### Complement Clauses

The first contentious category that Stede et al. (2017) discuss is "complement clauses," stating that "complement clauses do not constitute EDUs. These include subject and object clauses, and some objects of nouns and other parts of speech" (p. 5).

#### Subject Clauses

From the point of view of SFL, there are good reasons why "subject clauses" would not be treated as EDUs by Stede et al. (2017).

- **A subject clause is the so called "rankshifted" (embedded) clause.** In SFL, the structural hierarchy of language is organized on a rank scale (clause > group/phrase > word > morpheme). However, language has the potential for rank shift, which is a mechanism where a higher-ranking unit is downgraded to function inside a unit of its own rank or lower. So, when a clause acts as a Subject, it is "an embedded, down-ranked clause serving as Head" (cf.: Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 140) of a nominal group.
- **It acts as a participant IN a figure**. In the experiential structure of a clause, the Subject and Complement inherently represent **participants**. When a clause is rankshifted to serve as a Subject, the grammar is taking what would congruently be a whole figure and "packaging" it into a single element (a "macro-phenomenon"). Thus, the down-ranked clause functions strictly as a participant (such as Agent, Carrier, or Phenomenon) **within** the structural configuration of a single figure.
- **It is not a relation BETWEEN figures**. SFL draws a strict line between "tactic" relations (parataxis and hypotaxis) and embedding (rank shift):

	- **Tactic relations** (clause complexes) establish logico-semantic links **between** independent clauses, modeling a sequence of separate figures.
	- **Embedding** is **not** a relation between clauses, and thus not a relation between figures.

The authors state explicitly that "there is no direct relationship between an embedded clause and the clause within which it is embedded" (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 491). Because the embedded clause has been "swallowed up" by a nominal group to act as an internal participant, its relationship to the "outer" clause is entirely indirect.

Because an embedded subject clause acts functionally as an internal participant (a "thing") within a single figure, it does not exist on the same phenomenal plane as the primary clause. Therefore, it cannot establish the kind of rhetorical (or logico-semantic in SFL) relation that exists **between** separate figures in a sequence, completely justifying the decision not to segment it as a separate EDU in RST parsing.

This identical rationale applies to the GUM (eRST) framework. Because GUM inherits its baseline segmentation rules from the original RST-DT guidelines, it operates under the exact same strict constraint: "Subject and object clauses are not segmented". Therefore, both frameworks treat these rankshifted macro-phenomena as integral participants within a single EDU, rather than as separate spans available for rhetorical relations.

#### Object Clauses

In the Stede et al. (2017) RST guidelines, complement clauses of reporting verbs (like *he said that he was happy*) are not treated as Elementary Discourse Units (EDUs). The guidelines explicitly state that a direct object clause "is a unit of syntax, not of discourse" (p. 5).

An object clause of the type given as an example in the source ([I wouldn’t be far off] [if I said this is one of his greatest performances.]) in terms of SFL is a clause of projection.

In SFL, while **expansion** links figures that belong to the same first-order material environment (e.g., *Tarzan pulled the rope; so it broke*), **projection** bridges two entirely different orders of experience. It links a first-order phenomenon (the physical or mental act of saying/thinking) to a "second-order" semiotic reality (the metaphenomenon of what is actually meant or worded). Because the projected clause (*that he was happy*) exists on this different, semiotic plane, it does not establish a standard real-world rhetorical connection (like cause, sequence, or condition) with the primary clause. That would be one way to approach the explanation why such clauses are not EDUs.

SFL also explains exactly why Stede et al. (2017) view this as "syntax" rather than "discourse." In an indirect projection like *he said that he was happy*, SFL classifies the relationship as a **hypotactic report**. Because the projected clause is dependent (hypotactic) and belongs to a second-order reality, it loses its independent speech function (its mood and arguability). SFL explicitly notes that this gives the projected clause "more of an ideational status (cf. its treatment in traditional grammar as 'object' of the projecting verb)".

Because the projection downgrades the second figure into a dependent, second-order "object" of the first figure's consciousness or speech, it acts as an integrated syntactic complement rather than an independent rhetorical move. That would be the second way to explain why this is not an EDU.

**The "Attribution" Exception for Object Clauses.** We previously established that there are reasons why object clauses following cognitive or verbal verbs (projections) may not be segmented. However, GUM does segment these clauses either applying an `attribution` relation (when the reported speech or thought is actually the central message of the text) or an `elaboration-additional` (when the speech verb is more central to the logic of the node above and thus makes up the nucleus of the span it is part of; see the discussion in Zeldes et al., 2025, p. 35-36).

- **Example:** _[It was wonderful,] [Bill thought]_.
- **The SFL Lens:** GUM is essentially recognizing that in these cases, the projecting clause (*Bill thought*) is functioning interpersonally as a modal assessment or "Angle" on the information, rather than experientially as a core material event. Because the projected clause (*It was wonderful*) is the actual "first-order" message the writer wants to convey, GUM elevates it to an independent EDU.
- **Other variants:** GUM also applies this to cognitive phrases like _feel like_ (e.g., _[I feel] [like it's not always true]_), treating them as segmented attribution predicates.

This reflects the flip side of SFL theory: even though a reported clause acts syntactically like an object, projection still fundamentally creates a **clause nexus** (a complex of two distinct figures) rather than a single simple clause.

#### Attribute/Cognitive Verbs and *If/Whether* Clauses

Stede et al. (2017) also separately list verbs like *believe, know, say* (taking *that*-clauses) and *doubt, wonder* (taking _if/whether_ clauses) as not followed by EDUs.

- **The SFL Explanation:** In SFL, these are **mental (cognitive) and verbal clauses projecting ideas and locutions**. While *that*-clauses project propositions (statements of information), *if/whether* clauses project indirect questions, reflecting a mental state of an "undecided" mind (like wondering or doubting).
- **The RST Connection:** Just like the "object clauses" we discussed earlier, these projected clauses do not expand on a first-order material reality; they project a second-order semiotic reality (a meaning or a wording). Because the projected clause is dependent on the projecting consciousness or speech, it lacks the independent status required to form a rhetorical expansion relation with the primary clause.

As has been noted before, GUM makes an explicit exception for **attribution**. This applies equally to _that_-clauses representing propositions, and _if/whether_ clauses representing indirect questions.

#### Verbs that Take a Non-finite Clause

One area where Stede et al. (2017) and GUM align again is in their treatment of **hypotactic verbal group complexes**.

Stede et al. (2017) list verbs like *appear, attempt, try, decide, promise* followed by non-finite clauses (e.g., *I decided to leave*) as cases where no separate EDUs must be identified. GUM follows this exact same principle, stating that infinitival objects are kept as a single segment. This shared approach makes perfect sense from the point of view of SFL.

- **The SFL Explanation:** SFL treats many of these sequences not as two separate clauses, but as a **verbal group complex**. In a verbal group complex, two or more verbs are concatenated to function together as a **single Process** within a single clause. For example, in _she tried to do it_, the entire phrase "tried to do" acts as the Process realizing a single figure of experience.
- **The RST Connection:** Because these verb chains are unified into a single Process within a single grammatical clause, they represent only one "quantum of change" (one figure). Since rhetorical relations must hold _between_ separate figures, it, of course, makes sense not to split a single Process into multiple EDUs.

**Segmented (Adjunct Purpose).** If the _to_-infinitive acts as an adjunct explaining the hypothetical goal or intention behind an action, it triggers the **Purpose** relation and must be split off as a satellite, exactly the same as if the author had explicitly written "in order to". Through an SFL lens, this distinguishes between a secondary verb that is tightly bound into a single verbal group complex (not segmented) and a separate non-finite clause that acts circumstantially to enhance the main figure (segmented).

GUM perfectly matches the SFL concept of "verbal group complexes" too, stating that infinitival objects and modal-like verb chains are **not** segmented (with to-infinitive clauses being an exception again).

- **No segmentation:** _[I want to go]_ or _[She was capable of driving the car]_. These are treated as a single Process.
- **Segmentation for Purpose:** However, if the _to_-infinitive functions circumstantially as a purpose clause rather than as an object, it **is** segmented (e.g., _[We went to Turkey] [to enjoy some sunny weather]_).


#### Noun Complements ("the fact that...") and Relative Clauses

The authors (Stede et al. 2017) specify that clauses acting as complements to nouns (e.g., _the fact that these fantasy novels were marketed_) and defining relative clauses (e.g., _that caught my attention_) are not discourse units.

- **The SFL Explanation:** Both of these are classic examples of **rankshift (embedding)**.

    - **Noun complements:** A clause like _that these fantasy novels were marketed_ is a "fact" clause embedded as a Postmodifier (Qualifier) to the Head noun _fact_.
    - **Defining relative clauses:** A clause like _that caught my attention_ is an embedded elaborating clause, serving to specify the class of the Head noun (_the thing_).

- **The RST Connection:** As we established with subject clauses, embedding downgrades a clause so that it functions inside a nominal group. The embedded clause acts merely as a piece of a participant (a "thing"). Therefore, because they are swallowed up by the nominal group, they cannot participate in the tactical logico-semantic relations that bind separate figures together in discourse.

This part may also be where SLSeg and GUM diverge the most because GUM explicitly segments relative clauses, reduced relatives (participial clauses), and _to_-infinitives that modify nouns.

- **Examples:** _[We met the woman] [who lived in the house]_ or _[we had a chance] [to succeed]_.
- **The SFL Lens:** In SFL, these clauses are syntactically downgraded to act as mere modifiers within a nominal group (a participant). However, GUM chooses to semantically "unpack" this rankshift. GUM recognizes that, deep down, these embedded clauses still contain a Process, Participants, and Circumstances (they are still "figures" of experience). By segmenting them and assigning them relations like `elaboration-attribute` or `purpose-attribute`, GUM treats them as secondary figures that rhetorically elaborate on a participant in the primary figure.

Note, however, that GUM still refuses to segment prepositional phrases acting as modifiers (e.g., _[We met the woman in charge of the house]_) unless "the headword of the adnominal clause" is a verb, typically a gerund. Compare: _[They developed a method]_ _[for opening bottles]_ versus the unsegmented _[They developed a method for survival]_.

This distinction can be elegantly explained through SFL. In the first example, the gerund _opening_ remains a verb, meaning the unit is an embedded non-finite clause that still congruently realizes a figure of experience (a Process + Goal). Because it is a figure, GUM extracts it as an EDU. In the second example, however, the process of surviving has been 'incongruently' expressed as a noun (*survival*) via ideational grammatical metaphor. Because the figure has been entirely nominalized and stripped of its verbal status, GUM treats it purely as a syntactic participant and does not segment it (because even with the most "semantic" of approaches, the line has to be drawn somewhere).

### Relative Clauses

These have been already discussed above, but since Stede et al. (2017) discuss them in a dedicated subsection, we can follow a similar logic in our review.

The reason we might want to discuss them separately is because Stede et al. draw a hard line between restrictive (defining) and non-restrictive (non-defining) relative clauses. As before, SFL theory provides the exact grammatical reason for why this discourse boundary exists:

- **Restrictive Relative Clauses = Rankshift (Not EDUs):** As we established earlier, defining relative clauses are **rankshifted** (embedded). They function strictly inside a nominal group as a Postmodifier (Qualifier) to specify a subset of a class. Because they are grammatically downgraded to act merely as a "piece of a participant", as it were, within a single figure, they do not establish relations between separate figures. This perfectly matches Stede's intuition that they "are probably not units of discourse, merely restricting the meaning of a noun".
- **Non-Restrictive Relative Clauses = Hypotaxis (Are EDUs):** In SFL, non-defining relative clauses are **not embedded**. Instead, they are hypotactically dependent clauses. Because they are not swallowed up by a nominal group, they establish a true clause nexus (a tactic relation) with the primary clause, meaning they function as **independent figures in a sequence**.
- **Referring to a Proposition:** Stede notes that non-restrictive clauses "tend to refer to a discourse entity (a proposition)" (p. 6). SFL explicitly confirms this: while a defining relative clause restricts a noun, the domain of a non-defining relative clause **can be** a whole primary clause.

While Stede et al. strictly follow the SFL structural boundary (if it is rankshifted, it is not an EDU; if it is hypotactic, it is an EDU), GUM, as we have put it earlier, takes a more aggressive semantic approach that overrides this grammatical distinction.

- **GUM segments BOTH types:** GUM agrees with Stede on segmenting non-restrictive clauses, but it uniquely mandates the segmentation of defining/restrictive relative clauses as well.
- **Unpacking the Rankshift:** Through an SFL lens, GUM refuses to let the grammar "hide" a figure via rankshift. Even though a defining relative clause like *[the woman] [who lived in the house]* is syntactically embedded as a participant modifier, GUM recognizes that it still contains a Process and Participants. GUM extracts this embedded figure, segments it into an EDU, and assigns it an **`elaboration-attribute`** relation.

### Prepositional Adjuncts and Strong Discourse Markers

Stede et al. (2017) start their discussion of prepositional adjuncts by saying that "some prepositional adjuncts are good candidates for discourse status, as they are very close to clausal adjuncts" (p. 6).

Following this logic, the authors state that *"regardless of income"* is not an EDU in *Both students and faculty pay the same amount for childcare, regardless  of income*, whereas *"regardless of how good the  performance is"* **is one** in *It is hard to see past his megastar status, [regardless of how good the  performance is.]*.

In SFL, both these examples -- *regardless of income* and *regardless of how good the performance is* -- express the exact same logico-semantic relation of **concession** (a type of enhancing expansion).

However, they sit at different points on what SFL calls the scale of grammatical realization. SFL explicitly describes a "gradual loss of information... as one moves from the finite free clause to the prepositional phrase" (Halliday & Matthiessen, 2014, p. 490). And as such, the parsing decision suggested above is very easily explained and hardly involves any "judgement calls".

- **The first example above is a clause (External Augmentation):** The unit _regardless of how good the performance is_ contains a verb, meaning it has its own Process and Medium. SFL states that such clauses have the full textual, interpersonal, and experiential "semiotic weight" of a clause. Because it is a fully realized "figure" linked to another figure, it represents an _external_ expansion forming a clause complex. Therefore, the SLSeg parser is going to correctly recognize it as a separate EDU.
- **The second example is a prepositional phrase (Internal Augmentation):** SFL characterizes a prepositional phrase like _regardless of income_ as a "shrunken clause" or "minor clause". The preposition (_regardless of_) acts merely as a "minor process" (a mini-verb) and the nominal group (_income_) acts as an indirect participant. Because this unit lacks a true Process + Medium nucleus, the grammar has downgraded it to an _internal_ circumstantial element that merely augments a single figure. This perfectly explains why SLSeg (which requires a verb to identify an EDU) treats it as syntax rather than discourse.

While the SFL logic above perfectly justifies the SLSeg approach, the GUM (and RST-DT) guidelines actually reject the SLSeg rule for this specific example.

According to GUM, EDUs without a verbal predicate (like prepositional phrases) **are** segmented if they are introduced by a "Strong Discourse Marker". The guidelines provide an exhaustive list of these markers, which explicitly includes _because of, despite, according to,_ and **regardless**.

Therefore, in GUM, _[Both students and faculty pay the same amount for childcare,] [regardless of income]_ **would** actually be parsed as two separate EDUs.

- **The SFL Lens on GUM:** By mandating segmentation for strong markers like _regardless_, GUM is essentially deciding that the "minor process" (the preposition) carries so much rhetorical force that the "shrunken clause" must be unpacked. Even though there is no verb, GUM elevates the minor process back to the level of discourse, treating it as if it were a fully realized figure of concession.

While both parsing frameworks offer theoretically sound methodologies, the GUM/RST-DT approach provides a more functionally accurate model for discourse analysis. By segmenting verbless circumstantial phrases introduced by strong discourse markers, GUM successfully captures the writer’s rhetorical intent. In such constructions, the author deliberately deploys these markers as 'minor processes' to pragmatically frame or 'spin' the logico-semantic relationship between two phenomena. This persuasive strategy is entirely obscured if segmentation relies strictly on the formal syntactic presence of a verb.

## Conclusion: Syntax vs. Semantics

Ultimately, the divergence between the Stede et al. and GUM/eRST frameworks boils down to how strictly they map formal syntax to discourse. Stede et al. treat grammatical rankshift as a hard boundary: if a clause is embedded as a participant, it is locked inside a single figure of experience and cannot act as an independent discourse unit.

GUM, adopting a more "semantically aggressive" stance, willingly unpacks these rankshifted structures -- such as mental projections, defining relative clauses, and minor processes triggered by strong discourse markers -- whenever they functionally realize a distinct rhetorical move. SFL provides the perfect theoretical bridge for both approaches: validating Stede’s grammatical rigor while perfectly explaining GUM’s necessity to expose the underlying, multi-layered semantic reality of the discourse.
